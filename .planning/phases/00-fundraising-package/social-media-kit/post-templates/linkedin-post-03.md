## Post

Before writing a line of code, we mapped three federal compliance gates.

When you're building a platform that touches substance abuse treatment, healthcare privacy, financial disbursements, and decentralized identity -- legal diligence isn't optional. It's the foundation.

Here are the three compliance gates we identified before any engineering began:

1. Anti-Kickback Statute (AKS)
Our platform uses token-based incentives to encourage facilities and individuals to contribute verified data to a Decentralized Knowledge Graph. Under 42 U.S.C. 1320a-7b(b), any incentive tied to patient referrals carries exposure under the federal Anti-Kickback Statute. Our incentive structure must be reviewed by an AKS-specialized attorney before Phase 2 of development begins. We will not design the incentive mechanism until this review is complete.

2. 42 CFR Part 2 (Substance Use Disorder Privacy)
42 CFR Part 2 is stricter than HIPAA and applies to virtually every federally-assisted substance abuse treatment program. It prohibits even treatment-purpose disclosures without specific written patient consent. Our review verification flow -- where a facility confirms a reviewer's attendance -- must be designed with a healthcare privacy attorney to ensure zero patient-linkable data reaches our public layer. The Decentralized Knowledge Graph must hold no personally identifiable information, ever.

3. FinCEN / BSA (Crypto Disbursements)
Our funding model includes smart contract-based disbursements in stablecoins and cryptocurrency. Under FinCEN regulations, this may require Money Services Business (MSB) registration and state-level money transmitter licenses. We will not design the disbursement architecture until a FinCEN-specialized attorney has reviewed our structure.

Why does this matter?

Because in healthcare technology, "move fast and break things" breaks people. The substance abuse treatment space has been damaged by platforms that prioritized growth over compliance. Predatory patient brokering, fake reviews, and pay-to-rank schemes persist in part because the platforms that enabled them didn't do this work upfront.

We believe that mapping legal constraints before writing code is a signal of seriousness to funders, partners, and -- most importantly -- the communities we're building for.
